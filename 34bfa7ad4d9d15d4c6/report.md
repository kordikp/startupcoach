# Security testing and runtime protection for AI systems

**Founder:** @nohavond  ·  **Projekt:** startup — https://gitlab.fit.cvut.cz/nohavond/startup

> Can we develop an automated platform that (1) generates novel adversarial prompts to discover unknown vulnerabilities in deployed LLMs, (2) detects and prevents adversarial inputs in real time with very low latency and minimal false positives, (3) evaluates the security of LLM-based agents and their tool access, and (4) automatically maps security testing outputs to regulatory compliance artifacts? The research asks whether these capabilities can be achieved reliably given constraints on coverage, latency, verifiability and auditability.

**Tags:** `evolutionary adversarial prompt generation`, `low-latency adversarial detection`, `LLM agent security`, `regression security testing`, `automated compliance mapping`

### Regulatory Standards & Frameworks Driving AI Security Testing Demand
Five major regulatory frameworks create explicit demand for automated security testing and compliance evidence generation: (1) EU AI Act requires high-risk AI systems to achieve documented accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity standards with conformity assessment by August 2026 (now delayed to December 2027); (2) ISO/IEC 42001:2023 establishes mandatory AI management systems with white-box/grey-box/black-box testing requirements across the AI lifecycle; (3) NIST AI RMF 1.0 mandates security testing in the MEASURE function including adversarial testing and prompt injection resistance, with NIST AI 600-1 (July 2024) adding 12 generative AI-specific risk categories; (4) OWASP LLM Top 10 (2025 edition) identifies 10 critical vulnerabilities from prompt injection to model theft, requiring systematic adversarial testing; (5) MITRE ATLAS provides 16 tactics and 84 techniques for adversarial ML testing, including ML Model Access and ML Attack Staging tactics.
- EU AI Act Article 15 mandates high-risk AI systems achieve documented levels of accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity throughout lifecycle. Cybersecurity requirements specifically address AI-specific vulnerabilities: data poisoning, model poisoning, adversarial attacks, and confidentiality breaches. Conformity assessment required before deployment; enforcement deadline extended to December 2, 2027 (from August 2, 2026) per Digital Omnibus amendments. Non-compliance fines: up to EUR 15 million or 3% annual turnover for high-risk violations.
- ISO/IEC 42001:2023 (first international AI management system standard) requires organizations implement control objectives across 9 domains with 38 specific controls. Security testing requirements include white-box, grey-box, and black-box testing during development; biometric and continuous-learning systems require feedback-loop bias mitigation; STRIDE threat modeling framework maps security threats to lifecycle stages (Inception, Design/Development, Verification/Validation, Deployment, Operation/Monitoring, Re-evaluation).
- NIST AI Risk Management Framework 1.0 mandates organizations design tests and metrics under MEASURE function covering accuracy on representative queries, hallucination rates, security testing for prompt injection, and fairness evaluation. MANAGE function requires implementation of guardrails, content filters, human review, logging, and incident response. NIST AI 600-1 (Generative AI Profile, July 2024) extends core RMF with 12 risk categories including Information Security, Confabulation, Data Privacy, and Harmful Bias; provides 200+ suggested actions for governing generative AI risks.
- OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications (2025 edition, released late 2024) identifies 10 critical vulnerabilities requiring systematic testing: LLM01 Prompt Injection, LLM02 Insecure Output Handling, LLM03 Training Data Poisoning, LLM04 Model Denial of Service, LLM05 Supply Chain Vulnerabilities, LLM06 Sensitive Information Disclosure, LLM07 Insecure Plugin Design, LLM08 Excessive Agency, LLM09 Overreliance, LLM10 Model Theft. New 2025 entries: Vector/Embedding Weaknesses and System Prompt Leakage added after real-world incidents.
- MITRE ATLAS (Adversarial Threat Landscape for AI Systems) catalogs 16 tactics and 84 techniques (with 56 sub-techniques) for AI security red-teaming. Framework includes 13 tactics inherited from ATT&CK (Reconnaissance, Initial Access, Execution, Exfiltration) plus 2 AI-specific tactics: ML Model Access (inference API/artifact access exploitation) and ML Attack Staging (training data poisoning, backdoor insertion, adversarial example preparation). As of February 2026 v5.4.0: 32 mitigations and 42 real-world case studies published in STIX 2.1 format.
- EU AI Act Annex III identifies 8 categories of high-risk AI systems requiring security testing: Biometrics (remote ID, emotion recognition), Critical Infrastructure (traffic, utilities), Education (admissions, learning assessment), Employment (recruitment, performance evaluation), Essential Services (benefits, credit assessment), Law Enforcement (risk assessment, evidence evaluation), Migration/Border Control (visa/asylum evaluation), and Justice/Democratic Processes. Systems in these categories require mandatory conformity assessment.
- NIST AI RMF framework and NIST AI 600-1 guidance emphasize continuous, evidence-based monitoring. Organizations must track AI risks in production including performance metrics, safety, security, transparency, fairness, and environmental impacts. Testing tools recommended: adversarial robustness libraries, automated bias detection, model monitoring solutions. Prompt injection risk specifically maps to MEASURE testing and MANAGE incident response subcategories.
- Compliance evidence generation: All frameworks require documented testing results and technical files. EU AI Act requires instructions-of-use declarations of accuracy metrics and cybersecurity measures; ISO 42001 requires Clause 8.2 control implementation evidence and Clauses 9-10 continuous monitoring documentation; NIST RMF requires metric tracking across functions; OWASP and MITRE ATLAS provide standardized vulnerability categories enabling automated evidence mapping to regulatory categories.
